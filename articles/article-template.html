You Already Decided.
Logic Came After.

The decision was made in the first two seconds. Everything after — the pros-and-cons list, the research, the deliberation — was your brain's post-hoc justification engine running cover.

In 1983, Benjamin Libet ran an experiment that should have collapsed the way we talk about free will. Participants watched a clock, moved their wrist whenever they felt like it, and reported when the urge to move arrived. Libet's EEG readings showed the brain's readiness potential — the buildup toward movement — began 550 milliseconds before the participants consciously intended to act.

The implication was uncomfortable: the decision preceded the awareness of deciding. You were, in a measurable sense, the last to know.

The Rationalization Machine

The psychologist Jonathan Haidt calls this the social intuitionist model. His argument: moral judgments — and most judgments — are made intuitively, rapidly, and below conscious access. The reasoning we produce afterward isn't how we arrived at the conclusion. It's how we defend it to ourselves and others.

"The emotional tail wags the rational dog."

This isn't a character flaw unique to low-information people. In studies with law students, economics graduates, and trained statisticians, motivated reasoning shows up just as reliably — and in some cases more reliably, because high verbal intelligence produces more elaborate justifications.

Key Mechanism

The brain's primary function isn't accuracy. It's prediction and narrative coherence. Once a conclusion is formed, the reasoning system's job is to defend it — not to interrogate it. This is why "thinking harder" about a biased conclusion often makes the bias worse, not better.

What the Evidence Actually Shows

The split-brain experiments of Gazzaniga and Sperry gave us a window into this mechanism directly. When the corpus callosum is severed — disconnecting the brain's two hemispheres — the left hemisphere, which controls language and reasoning, will confabulate explanations for actions initiated by the right hemisphere that it cannot observe. It doesn't say "I don't know." It invents a reason. Confidently.

Gazzaniga called the reasoning-narrative system the Interpreter. Its job is not truth. Its job is coherence.

The consumer behavior data

Gerald Zaltman's decades of consumer research at Harvard suggest that roughly 95% of purchase decisions occur subconsciously. The stated reasons people give — price, features, timing — are often post-hoc rationalizations of choices that were already made at the emotional, associative level.

What This Actually Means For You

The practical implication isn't nihilism. Knowing about the Interpreter doesn't eliminate it — but it creates a gap. A pause where you can ask: is this reasoning, or is this defending?

The difference has a behavioral fingerprint. Reasoning seeks disconfirming evidence. Defense-mode avoids it, reframes it, or attacks the source. When you notice yourself explaining why something that contradicts your view "doesn't count" — that's the Interpreter working.

The Test

Ask: what evidence would change my mind? If you genuinely can't answer — or if the bar you set is impossibly high — you're not reasoning. You're rationalizing.

The goal isn't to distrust all intuition. Intuition encodes pattern recognition built over time. The goal is to stop mistaking the defense attorney in your head for the truth-seeking investigator.
